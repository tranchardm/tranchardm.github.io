"Reformulation and taxes for healthier consumption: Empirical evidence in the French Dessert market"

With Allais Olivier, Bonnet Céline, Requillart Vincent and Spiteri Marine


Noncommunicable diseases (NCDs) led to 74% of all deaths worldwide in 2019. Unhealthy diets are a major driver of NCDs. To discourage consumption of less healthy foods and promote a healthier food environment, many countries have implemented regulatory measures. Fiscal policy is one of these, as taxes have been successful in reducing the consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages. However, policymakers still have little guidance on how to design taxes to promote healthier diets and foods. The objective is to fill this gap by determining the potential equilibrium effects of a tiered sugar-based tax on unhealthy products on consumer purchases, nutrient consumption and welfare, firms' pricing decisions, and firms' profits when firms do or do not engage in product reformulation. In this paper, we develop a structural econometric model that incorporates consumers' substitution patterns across products, takes into account price competition among firms, and allows for the possibility that firms change the sugar content in response to taxation. Using household scanner data from the French dessert market, we show that the lower the tax threshold, the more efficient the tax policy in terms of sugar consumption reduction, but the larger the reduction in consumer surplus and profits. We also show that there is a certain level of tax threshold at which firms may experience less profit loss from the tax policy if they reformulate, thereby achieving both greater sugar consumption reduction and less consumer welfare loss.

"Strategic behavior in quality and price: Empirical evaluation of sin taxes in the french dairy market"

With Allais Olivier, Bonnet Céline and Spiteri Marine


Although we observe some reformulation effects due to the introduction of taxes (UK Government, 2018), the impact of a tax on product formulation is still neglected in the ex ante policy evaluation literature. In this paper, we develop a structural econometric demand and supply model that allows for both price and quality (or product formulation) responses to tax policy. Using the Kantar WorldPanel dataset on purchases of dairy desserts, combined with nutritional information from the Oqali dataset, we develop a marginal cost model that integrates all technological constraints in the production of dairy desserts. We extend the methodology of Barahona et al. (2023) by endogenizing product formulations on both the demand and supply sides.

"Are sin taxes well designed ?"

With Allais Olivier, Bonnet Céline and Leveneur Pauline


In recent years, the alarming rise in obesity rates and related health issues has been a concern among health experts and policymakers. Dietary habits are one of the main causes of obesity. So one potential solution to tackle this issue is the implementation of nutritional taxes (specifically targeting sugar content). Several fiscal measures have been implemented recently in Europe in order to influence consumer decisions towards better food alternatives. These taxes are called “sin taxes” because they target products considered harmful to society and individuals (such as alcohol, tobacco and soft drinks). Their aim is to reduce demand by raising prices and to generate revenue for the government. However, they are criticised for being paternalistic and regressive, given that these products are mainly consumed by the poor. Sin taxes are implemented to correct both internalitiesinternalities and externalitiesexternalities. Internalities arise because people consume certain products even though they may be harmful to their health. The reasons could be that consumers are imperfectly informed about the potential effects on their health or that they are time-inconsistent and underestimate how much they will value the health cost in the future1. Sugar consumption also generate externalities because the medical burden is also borne by other individuals in the society. The most important externality concerns the financial healthcare costs linked to the treatment of conditions caused by sugar consumption (e.g. weight gain, type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease). Therefore, it appears of primary interest to take into account externalities when designing nutritional taxes. In this paper, we start by carrying out ex-ante evaluations and comparisons of the effects of different tax designs (already existing or designed in the same spirit as existing ones) on the purchased quantity of non-alcoholic beverages and biscuits by households in the UK, France, and Spain following the methodology in Bonnet and Réquillart (2013).2 Then, we propose a new approach to design the optimal tax in terms of total welfare. To do so, we provide a regulatory framework where the regulator also takes into account the external costs of excessive sugar consumption. The optimal tax is defined on two parameters: the tax rates and the number of thresholds (in a stepwise function). To our knowledge, we are the first to propose this empirical approach to optimal taxation in the context of diet. Focusing on sugar-sweetened beverages, Allcott et al. 20193 propose a theoretical framework to identify when a tax is worthy and how different factors (e.g. the price elasticity of demand, externalities, internalities) determine the optimal tax.
